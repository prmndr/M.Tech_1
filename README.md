## Barriers to Learning

Barriers are often categorized into systemic, environmental, and individual factors.[cite: 1]

### 1. Limited Resources
This is a primary barrier highlighted in your syllabus.[cite: 1] It includes a lack of physical materials (textbooks, labs), digital tools, and infrastructure. When students do not have the necessary "guidance materials," the effectiveness of the pedagogy is severely diminished.[cite: 1]

### 2. Large Class Sizes
Large classes make it difficult for teachers to implement "Pedagogic strategies" like differentiated instruction or one-on-one support.[cite: 1] In these environments, teachers often default to lecture-based methods, which may not align with the "Theories of learning" that promote active engagement.[cite: 1]

### 3. Teacher Attitudes and Beliefs
If a teacher's internal beliefs are not aligned with modern pedagogical practices, they may resist new curriculum changes or inclusive teaching methods.[cite: 1] A lack of "Professional Development" or "Peer support" further reinforces outdated or ineffective teaching habits.[cite: 1]

### 4. Misalignment of Curriculum and Assessment
When the school curriculum and the methods of assessment are not synchronized, students focus on rote memorization rather than deep understanding.[cite: 1] This creates a "Research gap" between what is taught and what is actually learned.[cite: 1]

## Optimal Solutions and Examples

To overcome these barriers, pedagogy must move toward "Theory of change"—a process that transforms the educational environment.[cite: 1]

| Barrier | Optimal Solution | Practical Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Limited Resources** | **Open Educational Resources (OER) & Peer Support** | Instead of expensive textbooks, use digital repositories or community-sourced materials. Head teachers can facilitate "resource sharing" workshops.[cite: 1] |
| **Large Class Sizes** | **Collaborative & Peer Learning** | Implementing "Peer support" models where students work in small groups. This shifts the teacher's role from a lecturer to a facilitator.[cite: 1] |
| **Teacher Resistance** | **Continuous Professional Development** | Providing "follow-up support" after training sessions. Teachers observe peers and receive constructive feedback to build confidence in new strategies.[cite: 1] |
| **Rigid Curriculum** | **Contextualized Pedagogy** | Adapting guidance materials to fit the local context of the learners (e.g., using local language or culture to explain complex IT concepts).[cite: 1] |
